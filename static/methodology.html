Methodology

Last reviewed: June 2026

SleepWise is built around one rule: safety decisions are made by deterministic code, not by a language model. Here is how a result is produced.

1. Medication normalization

Your medications are mapped to drug classes (for example, lorazepam and Xanax both map to "benzodiazepine"). The matcher handles brand names, strips dosages from free text, and corrects obvious typos. A missed match is treated as a safety risk because it could hide a real interaction.

2. Deterministic safety engine

Each candidate supplement is checked against a hand-verified interaction table covering drug classes and health conditions. The engine returns one of three states: lower concern, use caution, or ask a clinician first. A second pass flags additive sedation when two or more sedating supplements would be combined. No language model is involved in this step.

3. Evidence retrieval

For each supplement, evidence is retrieved from a curated corpus drawn from public medical sources using a transparent keyword-ranking (BM25) method. Every claim shown carries a source link.

4. Plain-language explanation

An explanation is generated only from the facts the safety engine already approved. When a language model is used, it rewrites those vetted facts into clearer prose and is never allowed to add a new claim, dose, or interaction. The authoritative warnings always come from the engine.

How we check ourselves

An automated evaluation suite runs on every change and measures retrieval quality, whether known medication/supplement combinations get the expected outcome, and whether explanations stay faithful to their sources (including a check that no invented doses slip through). A change that weakens a known-dangerous combination fails the build.

Limitations

  • The interaction table is intentionally small and is partly pending source verification.
  • Coverage is limited to sleep supplements; absence of a warning is not proof of safety.
  • It has not yet been reviewed by a licensed clinician or pharmacist.
  • Live drug-database resolution (RxNorm) is planned but not yet in place.
